# HMRC Reporter — Community Engagement Playbook

> **Purpose:** Generate traffic and customers by genuinely helping Airbnb hosts, property managers, and UK landlords in online communities where they already hang out.
>
> **Who this is for:** Huw himself or a VA following these instructions.
>
> **Golden rule:** Help first, sell second. If you wouldn't say it to a friend at the pub, don't post it.

## 1. WHERE to Engage

## 2. HOW to Engage

### Tone & Approach

**Be the knowledgeable friend, not the salesperson.**

- Write like you talk. No corporate speak.
- Use plain English for tax concepts ("HMRC wants to know your rental income — here's what they need" not "Compliance obligations require disclosure of property income…")
- Acknowledge frustration before offering solutions
- Share personal experience where possible ("When I was figuring this out for my own properties…")
- If you don't know something, say so. Don't guess on tax advice.
- NEVER copy-paste the same comment across groups. Each response should be tailored.

### The 90/10 Rule

- **90% of your activity:** Pure helpful answers with zero mention of your product
- **10% of your activity:** Mentioning HMRC Reporter when it genuinely solves the posted problem

If you're not sure whether to mention the tool, don't. The trust you build with helpful answers is worth more than any single product mention.

### Example Comment Templates

#### Template 1: Answering an Airbnb Tax Question

> **Post:** "Do I need to declare my Airbnb income to HMRC?"
>
> **Response:**
> Yes, absolutely. If you're earning from Airbnb (or any short-term rental), you need to declare it on your Self Assessment tax return. HMRC gets data from Airbnb directly via the platform reporting rules, so they already know about your income.
>
> You'll need to report your rental income and can claim allowable expenses (cleaning, platform fees, utilities, insurance, etc.). If you're furnishing the property, look into the Replacement of Domestic Items relief.
>
> The tricky bit is usually pulling together all the data from Airbnb's CSV exports and making sure it matches what you're reporting — especially if you're also on Booking.com or Vrbo.

*Note: No product mention. Pure value. Builds trust.*

#### Template 2: When Someone Mentions Spreadsheet Struggles

> **Post:** "I've been doing my Airbnb tax return on a spreadsheet and it's doing my head in. 3 properties across 2 platforms and the numbers don't add up."
>
> **Response:**
> That's really common — Airbnb and Booking.com export data in completely different formats, and reconciling them manually is a nightmare. A few tips:
>
> 1. Make sure you're separating platform fees from your actual income (a common mistake)
> 2. Keep a separate tab for each property
> 3. Track check-in/check-out dates for the 90-day rule if you're in London
>
> Honestly though, with 3 properties across 2 platforms, you might want to look at something like [HMRC Reporter](https://hmrcreporter.com) — it pulls data from both platforms and generates the XML file HMRC needs for Making Tax Digital. Saved me hours of spreadsheet hell.

*Note: Product mention feels natural because it directly addresses the pain point.*

#### Template 3: MTD Confusion

> **Post:** "What is Making Tax Digital and does it apply to my Airbnb income?"
>
> **Response:**
> Making Tax Digital (MTD) for Income Tax is HMRC's move to digital record-keeping and quarterly reporting. It's being phased in — from April 2026, if your combined self-employment and property income is over £50k, you'll need to keep digital records and submit quarterly updates via MTD-compatible software.
>
> The threshold drops to £30k from April 2027, and there are plans to bring it lower after that.
>
> Key things to know:
> - You'll need MTD-compatible software (spreadsheets alone won't cut it)
> - Quarterly submissions replace the annual Self Assessment for qualifying income
> - You still do an end-of-year final declaration
>
> If you're already struggling with annual returns, now's the time to get your data organised. The quarterly cycle will be much less painful if your records are clean.

*Note: Expertise-first. No product mention. Commenter will remember the helpful person.*

#### Template 4: Penalty Worries

> **Post:** "I've just realised I haven't declared my Airbnb income for 2 years. Am I going to get fined?"
>
> **Response:**
> Don't panic — but do act on it. HMRC has a disclosure facility (the Digital Disclosure Service) where you can voluntarily declare undeclared income. Voluntary disclosure almost always results in lower penalties than if HMRC comes to you.
>
> You'll likely need to:
> 1. Submit returns for the missing years
> 2. Pay the tax owed plus interest
> 3. Potentially a penalty (typically 0–30% of the tax due, but voluntary disclosure usually gets the lower end)
>
> I'd recommend speaking to an accountant for this one, especially if the amounts are significant. The cost of an accountant is usually less than the penalties you'd avoid.
>
> Once you're caught up, staying on top of it going forward is much easier with the right tools.

*Note: Empathetic, practical, no selling. Exactly the kind of comment that gets saved/bookmarked.*

#### Template 5: When Directly Asked About Tools

> **Post:** "What software do people use for their Airbnb tax returns?"
>
> **Response:**
> There are a few options depending on your situation:
>
> - **Spreadsheets** — fine for 1 property, painful beyond that
> - **General accounting software** (Xero, FreeAgent, QuickBooks) — works but you still have to manually enter Airbnb data
> - **HMRC Reporter** (hmrcreporter.com) — specifically built for short-term rental hosts. It imports data from Airbnb, Booking.com, etc. and generates the XML file for HMRC. Especially useful if you're on multiple platforms or have multiple properties.
> - **Accountant** — always an option, but they'll still need your data organised
>
> The right choice depends on how many properties/platforms you're on and how much you want to DIY vs. outsource.

*Note: When the question is literally "what tool should I use?", it's fine to recommend directly. Frame it as one of several options.*

### What NOT to Do

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do Instead |
|---|---|
| Drop links without context | Only link when directly solving the posted problem |
| Copy-paste identical replies | Write fresh, tailored responses |
| Post "Check out my tool!" | Answer the question first, mention tool only if relevant |
| Argue with people | Disagree respectfully or move on |
| Post in groups you just joined | Lurk first, build credibility |
| Fake testimonials | Share real user stories (with permission) |
| DM people unsolicited | Only DM if they asked you to |
| Post purely promotional content | Ensure every post adds standalone value |

## 4. Weekly Routine

### Time Budget: 5–7 hours per week

This is enough to maintain meaningful presence across platforms without it becoming a full-time job. Scale up if you see results; scale down if certain platforms aren't converting.

### Weekly Schedule

| Day | Time | Platform | Activity | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Monday** | Morning | Facebook Groups | Check weekend posts, answer 3–5 tax questions | 45 min |
| **Monday** | Afternoon | Reddit | Browse r/AirBnB, r/UKPersonalFinance for relevant posts | 30 min |
| **Tuesday** | Morning | LinkedIn | Post or comment, engage with industry posts | 30 min |
| **Tuesday** | Afternoon | Property Forums | Check LandlordZone, Property118 for new threads | 30 min |
| **Wednesday** | Morning | Facebook Groups | Answer questions, check for trigger topics | 45 min |
| **Wednesday** | Afternoon | Reddit | Browse, answer, build karma | 30 min |
| **Thursday** | Morning | LinkedIn | Comment on 3–5 posts, share an insight | 30 min |
| **Thursday** | Afternoon | Mumsnet / Airbnb Forum | Check for relevant threads | 30 min |
| **Friday** | Morning | Facebook Groups | End-of-week sweep, answer remaining Qs | 30 min |
| **Friday** | Afternoon | — | **Metrics review** (see Section 5) | 30 min |

**Total: ~5.5 hours/week**

### Platform Priority Order

If you're short on time, prioritise in this order:

1. **Facebook Groups** — highest volume of relevant questions, most forgiving of tool mentions, best conversion potential
2. **Reddit** — highest quality traffic (people who click through are serious), but slower to build trust
3. **LinkedIn** — best for professional property managers and SA operators (higher-value customers)
4. **Property Forums** — niche but very targeted; Property118 is gold for tax questions
5. **Mumsnet** — lower volume but reaches part-time hosts who really need help
6. **Airbnb Community Forum** — relevant but Airbnb's own rules can limit engagement

### Scaling Up or Down

- **Got a VA?** Give them this playbook. Have them draft responses for your review for the first 2 weeks, then let them post directly once you trust their voice.
- **Too busy?** Drop to 3 hours/week. Focus on Facebook + Reddit only.
- **Seeing results?** Add Twitter/X engagement and consider creating a weekly LinkedIn newsletter.

## 5. Metrics & Tracking

### What to Track

#### Weekly Tracking Spreadsheet

Create a simple spreadsheet (Google Sheets works fine) with these columns:

| Column | What to Record |
|---|---|
| Date | When you posted |
| Platform | Facebook / Reddit / LinkedIn / Forum / Other |
| Group/Subreddit | Specific community |
| Post/Thread URL | Link to your comment |
| Type | Answer / Product Mention / Content Post |
| Trigger Topic | What the person was asking about |
| Engagement | Replies, upvotes, reactions |
| Clicks | (if trackable via UTM — see below) |
| Signups | Did this lead to a signup? (trace back if possible) |
| Notes | Anything useful to remember |

#### UTM Links for Tracking

Always use UTM-tagged links when sharing HMRC Reporter so you can see where traffic comes from in Google Analytics:

**Format:** `https://hmrcreporter.com?utm_source=[platform]&utm_medium=community&utm_campaign=[group_name]`

**Examples:**
- Facebook: `hmrcreporter.com?utm_source=facebook&utm_medium=community&utm_campaign=airbnb_hosts_uk`
- Reddit: `hmrcreporter.com?utm_source=reddit&utm_medium=community&utm_campaign=ukpersonalfinance`
- LinkedIn: `hmrcreporter.com?utm_source=linkedin&utm_medium=community&utm_campaign=post`
- LandlordZone: `hmrcreporter.com?utm_source=landlordzone&utm_medium=community&utm_campaign=forum`

Keep a master list of UTM links so you don't have to build them each time.

### Key Metrics to Review Weekly

| Metric | Target | How to Measure |
|---|---|---|
| Comments/posts made | 15–25 per week | Your tracking sheet |
| Platforms engaged | 3–5 | Your tracking sheet |
| Helpful answers (no product mention) | 80%+ of activity | Your tracking sheet |
| Product mentions | 2–4 per week | Your tracking sheet |
| UTM-tracked visits | Track trend (not absolute) | Google Analytics |
| Signups from community | Track trend | Cross-reference GA + signup data |
| DMs/conversations started | Track | Your inbox |

### Monthly Review (30 min, first Friday of the month)

1. Which platforms are driving the most traffic? (Check UTM data)
2. Which trigger topics get the most engagement?
3. Are any communities not worth the time? Drop them.
4. Are there new communities to join?
5. What questions keep coming up? → Consider making content about them (blog posts, LinkedIn articles)
6. Review your tracking sheet — look for patterns

### Red Flags to Watch For

- **Getting banned or warned:** You're being too promotional. Pull back and go pure-value for 2 weeks.
- **Zero engagement:** Your comments may be too long, too generic, or posted in dead threads. Study what gets responses in each community.
- **Traffic but no signups:** Your landing page may not match the promise. Review the funnel from community → site → signup.

## 6. Quick Reference Card

Print this or pin it somewhere visible:

```
BEFORE YOU POST, ASK:
1. Does this genuinely help the person who asked?
2. Would I say this even if I didn't have a product to sell?
3. Have I read the group/subreddit rules?
4. Am I adding to the conversation or just inserting myself?

IF YES TO ALL FOUR → post it.
IF UNCLEAR ON ANY → rewrite or skip.
```

```
PRODUCT MENTION CHECKLIST:
✓ They asked about tools/software
✓ They described a problem my tool solves
✓ I've already given helpful context first
✓ I'm framing it as ONE option, not THE option
✓ The group rules allow it
✓ I'm not repeating what I said in another comment on the same post
```

### B. Handling Difficult Situations

| Situation | How to Handle |
|---|---|
| Someone calls you a spammer | Apologise sincerely, explain you genuinely want to help, go pure-value for a while |
| Accountant disagrees with you | Defer to their expertise, but clarify if you were factually correct. Never get into a pissing match. |
| Someone has a complex tax issue | Recommend they speak to a qualified accountant. Don't give specific tax advice you're not qualified to give. |
| Competitor mentions your tool negatively | Don't engage publicly. If it's factually wrong, correct politely with evidence. |
| Group admin warns you | Thank them, apologise, ask what's acceptable. Follow their rules exactly going forward. |

### C. Disclaimer

This playbook is for community engagement — not professional tax advice. When engaging in communities:
- Never position yourself as a tax adviser
- Always recommend professional advice for complex situations
- Share general knowledge, not specific tax calculations
- When in doubt, say "I'd recommend speaking to an accountant about your specific situation"
