Do Airbnb Hosts Need to Report Income to HMRC?

Last updated: March 2026

Yes — in most cases, Airbnb hosts need to report their income to HMRC. The rules are straightforward, but many hosts still get them wrong. This guide explains exactly when you need to report, what triggers HMRC attention, and what happens if you don’t.

The Short Answer

If your gross Airbnb income exceeds £1,000 per year, you must report it to HMRC.

Gross income means the total booking amount before Airbnb deducts its fees. Not your payout — the full amount guests paid.

When You DON’T Need to Report

There’s one exception: the property income allowance.

If your total gross rental income from all sources (Airbnb, Booking.com, direct lets — everything) is £1,000 or less per year, you don’t need to:

  • Register for Self Assessment
  • Report the income
  • Pay tax on it

This is a tax-free allowance, not a threshold you deduct. If you earn £1,000 or less, it’s entirely tax-free.

Example

  • You rent your spare room on Airbnb for 2 weeks a year
  • Total gross income: £800
  • Action required: None. You’re under the £1,000 allowance.

But if that same room earned £1,200:

  • Action required: Report to HMRC (contact them or file Self Assessment)

When You MUST Report

Gross Income Over £1,000

If your total gross rental income exceeds £1,000, you have reporting obligations.

Gross Income £1,000–£2,500

Contact HMRC. They may be able to collect the tax through your PAYE tax code (if you’re employed) rather than requiring a full Self Assessment.

Gross Income Over £2,500

You must file a Self Assessment tax return with SA105 property income pages.

Gross Income Over £50,000

Making Tax Digital applies from April 2026. You’ll need MTD-compatible software and quarterly submissions.

How HMRC Knows

Many hosts think HMRC won’t find out about small Airbnb earnings. That changed in January 2024.

Digital Platform Reporting

Under OECD rules, Airbnb (and all major platforms) report your earnings directly to HMRC. This includes:

  • Your total gross income for the tax year
  • Number of bookings/transactions
  • Your identity and property details

HMRC receives this data automatically. They match it against:

  • Self Assessment returns
  • Bank account information
  • Other platform data

If you don’t report, or if your figures don’t match, HMRC will notice.

It’s Not Just Airbnb

Booking.com, VRBO, Expedia, and other platforms also report to HMRC. If you list on multiple platforms, HMRC has data from all of them.

What Happens If You Don’t Report

HMRC Will Contact You

If HMRC has data showing Airbnb income but no matching Self Assessment, they’ll send a letter asking you to:

  • Register for Self Assessment
  • Report your income
  • Pay any tax due

Penalties

If you should have been reporting but weren’t:

  • Late registration penalty: Up to 100% of the tax due
  • Late filing penalties: £100 initial penalty, increasing over time
  • Late payment penalties: 2% at 15 days, additional 2% at 30 days, 4% annualised after that
  • Interest: Charged on unpaid tax from the original due date

Deliberate vs Careless

HMRC distinguishes between:

  • Careless errors: You didn’t realise you needed to report — penalties up to 30% of tax due
  • Deliberate errors: You knew but didn’t report — penalties up to 70% of tax due
  • Deliberate and concealed: You tried to hide income — penalties up to 100% of tax due

Common Misconceptions

“Airbnb handles the tax for me”

No. Airbnb reports your income to HMRC, but doesn’t file your tax return or pay your tax. That’s your responsibility.

“I only rent occasionally”

Doesn’t matter. If your gross income exceeds £1,000 in a tax year, you need to report.

“It’s just a hobby, not a business”

HMRC doesn’t care whether you call it a business or a hobby. Income is income.

“I’m under the personal allowance”

The personal allowance (£12,570) applies to your total income from all sources. If your employment income uses up your personal allowance, Airbnb income is taxed on top.

“Cash payments don’t count”

All income counts — whether paid through Airbnb, bank transfer, or cash.

What You Need to Report

When you do report, you need:

  • Gross income from Airbnb (and other platforms)
  • Allowable expenses (cleaning, maintenance, insurance, platform fees, etc.)
  • Property details (address, ownership)
  • Rental days and personal use days

How to Report

Option 1: Self Assessment

  1. Register for Self Assessment (if not already registered)
  2. Complete SA105 property income pages
  3. Enter gross income and expenses per property
  4. Submit by 31 January following the tax year

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do I need to report if I earn less than £1,000? A: No. The property income allowance means income under £1,000 gross is tax-free.

Q: What if I didn’t know I had to report? A: Contact HMRC and register for Self Assessment. They’re usually lenient with first-time issues if you come forward voluntarily.

Q: Does the £1,000 allowance apply per property? A: No. It’s a total across all properties and all rental platforms combined.

Q: What if I also rent on Booking.com? A: You must combine income from all platforms. The £1,000 allowance covers your total gross rental income.

Q: Can I claim expenses against my Airbnb income? A: Yes. Allowable expenses (cleaning, maintenance, insurance, platform fees, etc.) reduce your taxable income. But the £1,000 allowance is based on gross income, not profit.
