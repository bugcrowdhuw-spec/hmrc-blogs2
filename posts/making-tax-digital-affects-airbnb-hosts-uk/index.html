How Making Tax Digital Affects Airbnb Hosts in the UK

Last updated: March 2026

If you’re an Airbnb host in the UK, Making Tax Digital will change how you report your income. Gone are the days of one annual Self Assessment. Instead, you’ll submit quarterly updates to HMRC using digital software. Here’s what Airbnb hosts specifically need to know.

The Short Version

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax requires you to:

  1. Keep digital records of your Airbnb income and expenses
  2. Submit quarterly summaries to HMRC
  3. Use MTD-compatible software
  4. File a final declaration after the tax year

When it applies to you depends on your total property income.

MTD Dates for Airbnb Hosts

Your property incomeMTD starts
Over £50,000/yearApril 2026
Over £30,000/yearApril 2027
Over £20,000/yearExpected 2028

If you earn less than £20,000 from property, you’re not affected yet — but MTD is expected to expand to all property income.

Why Airbnb Hosts Face Extra Challenges

MTD is straightforward for landlords with one long-term tenant and predictable monthly rent. Airbnb hosts face a different reality.

High Transaction Volume

A single property on Airbnb might generate 50–100 bookings per year. Each one is a separate transaction. Multiply that by several properties and you’re dealing with hundreds of data points.

Multiple Platforms

Most Airbnb hosts also list on Booking.com, VRBO, or other platforms. Each platform exports data differently. MTD requires you to consolidate all income digitally.

Variable Income

Airbnb income fluctuates with seasons, events, and demand. Quarterly submissions need to capture these variations accurately.

Gross vs Net Confusion

Airbnb pays you after fees. HMRC wants gross income. You need software that handles this distinction automatically — or you’ll be manually adjusting every quarter.

What MTD Means for Your Airbnb Records

Before MTD

  • Download Airbnb CSVs once a year
  • Enter totals into Self Assessment
  • Submit by 31 January

After MTD

  • Keep digital records continuously
  • Submit quarterly summaries (April–June, July–September, October–December, January–March)
  • File a final declaration after the year ends
  • All records must be digital and MTD-compatible

What You Need to Track Digitally

Per property, per quarter:

  • Gross income from Airbnb (and other platforms)
  • Platform fees
  • Allowable expenses (cleaning, maintenance, insurance, etc.)
  • Rental days vs personal use days
  • Property details and ownership

How Quarterly Submissions Work

Quarter 1: April – June

Submit a summary of income and expenses for April, May, and June. Deadline: 7 August.

Quarter 2: July – September

Submit for July, August, September. Deadline: 7 November.

Quarter 3: October – December

Submit for October, November, December. Deadline: 7 February.

Quarter 4: January – March

Submit for January, February, March. Deadline: 7 May.

End of Period Statement

After the tax year ends, submit a final statement confirming your totals. This is where you reconcile your quarterly submissions with your actual annual figures.

Final Declaration

A final submission combining all income sources (property, employment, other) and calculating your total tax bill.

How to Prepare as an Airbnb Host

Step 1: Check Your Income

Calculate your total property income (before expenses). Include Airbnb, Booking.com, VRBO, and any other rental income. If it’s over £50,000, MTD applies from April 2026.

Step 2: Get MTD-Compatible Software

You need software that:

  • Imports Airbnb data (CSV or API)
  • Tracks income at property level
  • Handles expenses
  • Submits quarterly updates to HMRC

HMRC Reporter handles Airbnb data import and supports MTD requirements for short-term rental hosts.

Step 3: Start Keeping Digital Records Now

Don’t wait for the deadline. Start tracking digitally now so you’re comfortable with the process before it’s mandatory.

Step 4: Organise Your Properties

Make sure you have:

  • Each property’s address and details
  • Airbnb listing linked to the correct property
  • Income and expenses categorised properly

Step 5: Practice Quarterly Reporting

Run a practice quarter. Calculate your income and expenses for a three-month period and check that your software captures everything.

Can I Still Use a Spreadsheet?

Technically, you can use a spreadsheet connected to MTD bridging software — but it’s not recommended for Airbnb hosts. The complexity of:

  • Multiple platforms
  • High transaction volumes
  • Gross vs net calculations
  • Currency conversions

…makes spreadsheets impractical. Purpose-built software saves time and reduces errors.

The Cost of Getting MTD Wrong

MTD uses a points-based penalty system:

  • Late quarterly submissions earn penalty points
  • Accumulated points trigger a £200 fine
  • Persistent failures lead to higher penalties
  • Incorrect data can trigger HMRC investigations

Since HMRC already has your Airbnb income data (via Digital Platform Reporting), discrepancies between your submissions and their records will be flagged.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does MTD replace Self Assessment for Airbnb income? A: Yes. Once MTD applies to you, quarterly submissions and a final declaration replace the annual Self Assessment for property income.

Q: What software do I need for MTD? A: You need MTD-compatible software that can submit directly to HMRC. For Airbnb hosts, look for software that also imports platform data. HMRC Reporter does both.

Q: Can my accountant submit MTD updates for me? A: Yes, but you still need to keep digital records. Your accountant can submit on your behalf using MTD-compatible software.

Q: What if I only rent on Airbnb occasionally? A: If your total property income is under the threshold (£50,000 for now), MTD doesn’t apply to you yet. But it will eventually — all property income is expected to be covered by 2028.

Q: Do I need to submit every quarter even if I have no income? A: Yes. You must submit a quarterly update even if it shows zero income for that period.

Q: Does MTD apply to my Furnished Holiday Let? A: Yes. MTD applies to all property income, including holiday lets and short-term rentals.
